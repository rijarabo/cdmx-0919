Mexico is one of the world's most seismically active regions, sitting atop several intersecting tectonic plates.

Mexico City, itself, is exceptionally vulnerable to damage due to the fact that it is largely built over a dried lakebed.

According to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, "these soft sedimentary clay deposits amplified the seismic waves, or they liquefied, destroying the foundation of some buildings" during the 1985 quake.

A result of being built on a sinking lake bed, the city and its buildings will always be vulnerable to collapse.


For many citizens of the capital, September 19 has become a synonymous of disaster.

On 1985 two 8-magnitude earthquakes stroke the city causing serious damage to the Greater Mexico City area and the deaths of at least 5,000 people.

32 years after On 2017 , after the 1985 quake was commemorated and the traditional national earthquake drill was held, a 7.1-magnitude earthquake struck the city once again.


The toll of the 2017 earthquake in Mexico City was not as severe as 32 years ago, but the damages are still important:

  • Around 220 people where killed.
  • At least 44 buildings collapsed in Mexico City due to the earthquake, trapping people inside, creating large plumes of dust, and starting fires.
  • Between 50 to 60 people were rescued by emergency workers and citizens.
  • Several buildings caught fire. Condesa, Roma and del Valle neighbourhoods were among those most affected.
  • Mexico City International Airport suspended operations while damage assessments took place, but reopened at 4:00 p.m. CDT (2100 UTC). 180 flights were cancelled or diverted during the closure.

Help!

There are many ways to help victims of this and other natural disasters.

Here is a list of different organizations that help, or search directly for the following:

  • Mexican Red Cross: Red Cross shelters, feeds and provides emotional support to victims of disasters; supplies about 40 percent of the nation's blood; teaches skills that save lives; provides international humanitarian aid; and supports military members and their families.
  • Topos volunteer rescuers: An unpaid force of specialists, they work closely with firefighters and paramedics. They have earned a reputation worldwide, and have assisted after earthquakes in Japan (1995), Colombia (1999), Haiti (2010) and Nepal (2015), among others, and in Indonesia after the 2004 tsunami.
  • Oxfam México: Humanitarian experts team, has launched a Mexico earthquake appeal
  • Cadena: The Committee for Disasters Relief and National Emergencies (CADENA), is a non-profit civic association focused on preventing nd assisting on emergencies and natural disasters.
